UVA ASTRONOMY


FORGING A STRONGER MISSION


Transitioning Your Exercise Routine from Earth to Mars

SO YOU WANT TO TRAIN LIKE AN ASTRONAUT?

If you’re reading this, then you probably want to be an astronaut. Whether it’s a childhood fantasy as you played with toy rockets with friends or a legitimate pursuit involving years of study and work, who doesn’t want to be an astronaut?

But there’s a lot that goes into being an astronaut that doesn’t always get media coverage. Did you know that astronauts on the International Space Station have to work out at least two hours a day, six days a week? If they didn’t, the microgravity environment would lead to a serious loss in bone and muscle, and any number of long term heart issues. Did you know that NASA has to specially design the exercise equipment to work in microgravity? You can only run a treadmill for so long without floating away, after all. Luckily for you, your treadmill is safe and sound here on Earth, and ready to help you train like an astronaut on a mission to Mars.

WHY WE NEED TO WORKOUT IN SPACE

Humans are designed to live with gravity. Gravity is one of the only consistent things across all of human evolution, so as a species, we’re really good at living in Earth-like gravity. Microgravity on the other hand, not so much. When in orbit on the ISS, the astronauts experience so little gravity that for our intents and purposes, it doesn’t exist. This makes it much easier to move around, as you can just kinda fling yourself from room to room. As much fun as that may be, it can cause some serious health issues.

Your heart is used to pumping against gravity all the time, and it’s gotten pretty good at it. You, standing on Earth, get good consistent blood flow from your head to your toes because your heart has spent all it’s life fighting gravity. Now, when you get into orbit, your heart is no longer fighting gravity. For a six-month ISS mission, that’s six months of your heart not needing to fight gravity, six months of your heart getting weak. As it turns out, working out is really good at keeping your heart strong and healthy. By forcing your heart to pump at higher rates just to keep up with your oxygen consumption, you can keep your heart from atrophying.

The rest of your muscles face the same problem. If they don’t get used, they don’t need to exist, and astronauts can end up losing 15% of their muscle mass from just six months in space. Working out the level astronauts on the ISS train is a very effective way to prevent this atrophy. The combination of running, biking, and lifting keeps all your major muscles engaged and working at a similar level to on Earth.

Lastly, your bones also face issues with atrophy in microgravity. Bones, however, tend to atrophy due to lack of impact more than anything else. Just walking around on Earth causes stress to your bones, which then heal stronger than they were before. If bones don’t have these constant stresses and opportunities to heal, astronauts can end up dealing with osteoporosis and other long-term bone issues. However, impact-based exercise, like running and certain lifts, can be used to combat this.

HISTORY OF EXERCISE IN SPACE

  1. Resistive Exercise Device (RED) :
    The RED (and it’s newest model, the advanced RED or aRED) is an all-purpose lifting machine. The problem with most lifting equipment in space is that there is no gravity. It doesn’t matter if a dumbbell weighs 100 kilograms on Earth, because in orbit it will weigh next to nothing. And as cool as it would be to just be casually curling 100 kilos anytime, it doesn’t get you a workout. The solution? A machine that uses vacuums to simulate weight!

    The aRED uses adjustable vacuum cylinders to allow astronauts to change how much “weight” they are “lifting”. The device can be set up in one of more than half a dozen configurations, depending on which exercise the astronauts are doing. aRED also tracks the astronauts progress. With a tablet hooked up to the machine, every lift an astronaut does is measured and recorded. This allows both the astronauts themselves to track and progress, and Mission Control can check in and make sure they’re actually lifting enough.

    The astronauts use aRED daily, or else they risk permanently losing muscle mass. For your training plan, we won’t be doing that. aRED is a lifting device, yes, but more than that it’s meant to simulate what it’s like to exist in an environment with gravity. Given that you live in a gravitationally stable environment, you don’t need to lift as often as the astronauts do.
